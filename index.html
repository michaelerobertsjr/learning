Who has learned something new today? I'd like to talk to you briefly about a topic that is relevant to anyone learning a new skill. We're going to talk about how to acquire skills more effectively. Or if you will: learning how to learn.

Growth Mindset

  • Growth mindset: you can improve your skills with effort
  • Recognize that struggling helps you learn
  • Make sure you struggle and know that it helps
  • "If you think you can or canâ€™t, you are right."
- I don't have the skills that I do because I'm brilliant - I have the skills that I do because I acquired them over time through work, play, and help from others - When you have a **growth mindset** you know that you *can* learn the skills you want to learn. It will require effort, but you **absolutely** can do it. - Recognizing that struggling *helps* you learn actually *allows* struggling to help you learn. - I'm going to say that again. - Struggling helps you learn, but only if you recognize that struggling helps you learn. - So make sure you struggle and know that struggling helps learning.
- You want to make sure you're working at the edge of your ability: not too far off the edge, but *not* inside your comfort zone - If you're completely comfortable, you're not learning - But if you're completely overwhelmed, you're not going to learn either - In order to learn, you need to continually keep yourself *just* outside of your comfort zone - Take on tasks that you can't do on your own yet, but you might be able to do with a bit of Googling and asking for help - If you take little steps just beyond your abilities everyday, you will learn and you will grow

Active Retrieval

  • Learning happens from retrieval, not consumption
  • Quizzing yourself is more effective than re-reading
  • Quiz yourself early and quiz yourself often
- Have you ever heard people say "practice makes perfect"? - There's a reason they say that. - Practice isn't about putting information in your brain - Practice is all about retrieving information from your brain - Obtaining information is easy - Retrieving information is hard - **testing yourself** on what you've learned so far is *very* often **more useful** than re-reading** or re-watching material - Test yourself at the end of all learning. If you consume information and you fail to test yourself on what you learned afterward, you will not learn.

Active Retrieval Example

  1. Try to solve a programming exercise
  2. Don't look at the exercise for at least 24 hours
  3. Look at solutions for the exercise and reflect on them
  4. Don't look at the exercise for at least 24 hours
  5. Try to solve the exercise again and repeat from step 2
- Here's an example of one way to practice active retrieval - You could try to solve a programming exercise... it doesn't matter whether you do solve, it matters that you try to solve it and you struggle while doing so - And then step away from it, for a day - And when you come back look at solutions to the exercise and reflect on what you see - What's new to you in the answers? What can you figure out from them? - If you solved the exercise, how could you have solved it *better*? - And then step away again, for at least a day - And try to solve the same exercise again, without looking at your previous attempts or the solutions

Interleaved Practice

  • Blocked practice: practice one skill repeatedly
  • Interleaved practice: work on multiple skills in parallel
  • Blocking feels effective, but interleaving is effective
  • Mix together different skills when practicing
- When you practice the same thing over and over, you'll feel smarter. But you're tricking yourself! - It's more effective to mix up your learning - Come up with different chunks of stuff and then mix them together - Try not to practice the same skill twice in a row - Don't work on one topic all day: do a little of bit Django models - It would be better to practice Django models, templates, and views all intermixed

Spaced Practice

  • don't cram: space out your practicing
  • take breaks, go for a walk
  • pause in the middle of learning topics
- Who has watched an entire season of Netflix in a day? That's binge-watching. - When I was in school I tried to binge-learn: I crammed for exams - Cramming doesn't work. When you cram, you may remember something for a short period but you won't retain that knowledge. - While learning, you want to space out your practice - Don't try to binge-learn everything in a weekend - If possible, set aside a *little* of time for practice every day - And if you have to learn in big blocks of time, make sure you take lots of breaks - Every 25 minutes: stop what you're doing and take a five minute break

Elaboration

  • Elaboration: describing an idea in your own words
  • Try explaining an idea to someone else in simple terms
  • Give a lightning talk on something you learned recently
  • Make connections between ideas whenever possible
- Have you ever heard someone say the best way to learn is to teach? - When you explain a complex idea in your own words, you're elaborating - Explaining how things relate to each other is also elaboration - Whenever you reframe an idea or connect multiple ideas, you're strengthening your learning - **Analogies** are one of my favorite ways to re-enforce learning - Take a concept you're learning and try to think of something in the real world that it's similar to and explain that concept using that real world thing - You've just made an analogy - If you use an analogy to explain a programming concept to someone who's never programmed before, you are re-enforcing your learning through elaboration

Recap: How to Learn

  • Retrieve: quiz yourself as often as possible
  • Growth mindset: know that struggling helps
  • Interleave: practice different skills at the same time
  • Spacing: remember to take breaks
  • Elaborate: make connections between different ideas
- Let's do a recap - The way to stop your brain from *forgetting* new ideas is to **actively retrieve** those ideas from your brain by quizzing yourself - As you quiz yourself, make sure you're struggling and know that **struggling helps learning** - Don't practice one skill over and over: **interleave** different skills when you practice - Take breaks often: don't binge-learn and don't cram - **Practice elaboration**. Explain an idea to a friend without using fancy programming terms... only use *plain English* when you're explaining things - Make connections between different ideas and make *lots* analogies - Analogies are fun, silly, *and* productive learning tools
