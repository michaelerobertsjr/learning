Learning Programming

Trey Hunner / @treyhunner

This talk is aimed at new programmers, but the topics I'm discussing are relevant for anyone learning a new skill. We're going to learn about learning.

Growth Mindset

  • Growth mindset: you can improve your skills with effort
  • Recognize that struggling helps you learn
  • Make sure you struggle and know that it helps
  • "If you think you can or canâ€™t, you are right."
- I don't have the skills that I do because I'm brilliant - I have the skills that I do because I acquired them over time through work, play, and help from others - When you have a **growth mindset** you know that you *can* learn the skills you want to learn. It will require effort, but you **absolutely** can do it. - Recognizing that struggling *helps* you learn actually *allows* struggling to help you learn. - I'm going to say that again. - Struggling helps you learn, but only if you recognize that struggling helps you learn. - So make sure you struggle and know that struggling helps learning.

Active Retrieval

  • Learning happens from retrieval, not consumption
  • Quizzing yourself is more effective than re-reading
  • Quiz yourself early and quiz yourself often
- Have you ever heard people say "practice makes perfect"? - There's a reason they say that. - Practice isn't about putting information in your brain - Practice is all about retrieving information from your brain - Obtaining information is easy - Retrieving information is hard - **testing yourself** on what you've learned so far is *very* often **more useful** than re-reading** or re-watching material - Test yourself at the end of all learning. If you consume information and you fail to test yourself on what you learned afterward, you will not learn.

Interleaved Practice

  • Blocked practice: practice one skill repeatedly
  • Interleaved practice: work on multiple skills in parallel
  • Blocking feels effective, but interleaving is effective
  • Mix together different skills when practicing
- When you practice the same thing over and over, you'll feel smarter. But you're tricking yourself! - It's more effective to mix up your learning - Come up with different chunks of stuff and then mix them together. - If you're solving programming exercises, a good strategy is to randomize exercises that use different skills. - Try not to practice the same skill twice in a row.

Spaced Practice

  • don't cram: space out your practicing
  • take breaks, go for a walk
  • pause in the middle of learning topics
- When I was in School I crammed for many tests - Cramming doesn't work. It remembered what I needed on the test, but forgot it very quickly afterward. - Remember to interleave your practice... you want to practice multiple things at once - so don't take break between topics, take breaks in the middle of topics - Take breaks at unnatural times: say every 25 minutes take a 5 minute break

Elaboration

  • Elaboration: describing an idea in your own words
  • Try explaining an idea to someone else in simple terms
  • Give a lightning talk on something you learned recently
  • Make connections between ideas whenever possible
- Have you ever heard someone say the best way to learn is to teach? - When you explain a complex idea in your own words, you're elaborating - Explaining how things relate to each other is also elaboration - Whenever you reframe an idea or connect multiple ideas, you're strengthening your learning - **Analogies** are one of my favorite ways to re-enforce learning - Here's some analogies: - **Caching** and expiration dates on food - **Generators** and watching a YouTube video - **Asynchronous programming** and planning a birthday party

Takeaways

  • Retrieve: quiz yourself as often as possible
  • Growth mindset: know that struggling helps
  • Interleave: practice different skills at the same time
  • Spacing: remember to take breaks
  • Elaborate: make connections between different ideas
- Stop your brain from *forgetting* new things by **actively retrieving** things from your brain - As you quiz yourself, make sure you're struggling and know that **struggling helps learning** - Don't participate in blocked practice: **interleave** different skills when you practice - Take breaks often, but make sure you're interleaving - **Practice elaboration**. Explain an idea to a friend without using fancy software engineering and computer science terms... no polymorphism, no scope, no coupling: just *plain English* - Make connections between different ideas and make *lots* analogies - Analogies are fun, silly, *and* productive learnings - And most importantly, give a lightning talk at the next San Diego JavaScript on a new thing you've learned and explain the idea in your own words
